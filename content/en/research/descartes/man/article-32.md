### 32. How do the spirits enter into the nerves and create motion?

 <!-- consider the motions dependent on them.  -->

If, in the innermost part of the brain, some of the tubes are not more open or in a different manner than others, and consequently, if the particular spirits do not have the impression of a certain idea, they are dispersed haphazardly and indifferently in all directions and pass through the pores around B into those around C. 

Hence, the finer particles of them will flow out completely through the pores of the membrane that envelops it, while the remaining parts of the spirits flowing towards D will pass into the nerves and muscles without producing any particular effect in them, as they are evenly distributed to all. 

However, if some of those tubes, due to the action of the object on the sensing agent, are more or less open, or are opened differently than their neighbors, the filaments composing the substance of the brain (therefore some being slightly more intense or relaxed than others) will lead the spirits to a certain part of their base, and from there into certain nerves with greater or lesser force than others. This is sufficient to stimulate different movements in the muscles, as explained more extensively above.
