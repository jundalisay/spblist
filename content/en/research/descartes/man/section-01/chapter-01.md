The body is a machine of earth which God forms on purpose to make it as similar to us as possible. He gives its outside the color and shape of all our members, but he also puts on its inside the color and shape of our bodies. 

These parts make the body walk, eat, breathe, and finally imitate all those of our functions which can be imagined to proceed from matter, and depend only on the disposition of the organs.

It seems to me that I cannot imagine so many kinds of movement in this one, which I suppose to be made by the hands of God, nor attribute to it so much artifice, that you do not have reason to think that there may be even more.

First of all, the meats are digested in the stomach of this machine by the force of certain liquors.

These liquors slip between their parts, separate them, agitate them, and heat them, just as common water does those of quicklime, or strong water those of metals,

These liquors, brought from the heart by the arteries, become very hot.

Even the meats are such, usually, that they could corrode and heat up by themselves, just as new hay does in the barn when it is squeezed before it is dry.

The agitation that the small parts of these meats receive as they heat up, combined with that of the stomach and the intestines that contain them, and with the arrangement of the small threads of which these intestines are composed, means that as they digest, they descend little by little towards the conduit through which the coarser parts of them must leave; that prevents the bran from following it. 

These subtler parts of the meats are uneven and still imperfectly mixed together. These make up a liquor which would remain all hollow and whitish, were it not for the fact that a part of it mingles incontinently with the mass of blood, which is contained in all the branches of the vein called the portal vein (which receives this liquor from the intestines), in all those of the vein called the vena cava (which conducts it to the heart), and in the liver, as well as in a single vessel.

The pores of the liver are so arranged that, when this liquor enters it, it becomes subtle, elaborates itself. It takes on its color and acquires the form of blood, just as the juice of black grapes, which is white, is converted into clear wine when it is left to ferment on the grater.

This blood thus contained in the veins has only one manifest passage through which it can leave, namely that which leads it into the right side of the heart.

The flesh of the heart contains in its pores one of those lightless fires of which I have spoken above, which makes it so hot and burning. As blood enters any of the 2 chambers in it, it swells rapidly and expands. This is like what happens when you pour the milk from any animal drop by drop into a very hot vessel.

The fire which is in the heart of the machine that I describe to you serves there for another thing than to dilate, warm and subtilise the blood, which falls continuously drop by drop, by a pipe of the vena cava in its right cavity from where it exhales in the lung. The flesh of the lung is so rare and so soft. Respiration serves only to thicken these vapors. It is necessary to maintain this fire.

## The Heart

Babies who are still in their mothers' wombs cannot draw any fresh air by breathing. They have 2 conduits which make up for this defect:
1. One through which the blood of the vena cava passes into the vein called the artery
2. The other through which the vapors or rarefied blood of the artery called the vein are exhaled and go into the great artery

Animals which have no lungs at all have only one cavity in the heart.
- If they have several, they are all consecutive to each other.

The pulse, or beat of the arteries, depends on the 11 little skins. These are like little doors which close and open the entrances of the 4 vessels which look into the 2 cavities of the heart.

When one of these beats ceases, another is about to begin. At that moment:
- the little doors which are at the entrances of the 2 arteries are exactly closed
- those which are at the entrances of the 2 veins are opened

In this way, 2 drops of blood fall through these 2 veins, one in each cavity of the heart. 

These drops of blood then become rarefied, and suddenly spread out into a larger space without any reason than the one they occupied before, pushing and closing those little doors which are at the entrances of the 2 veins.

This prevents any more blood from going down into the heart, and pushing and opening those of the two arteries, through which they enter promptly and with effort, thus making the heart and all the arteries of the body swell at the same time. 

But immediately afterwards, this rarefied blood condenses again, or enters the other parts; and so the heart and arteries swell up, the little doors at the two entrances of the arteries close again, and those at the entrances of the two veins open again, and give passage to two other drops of blood, which again swell up the heart and arteries, just as the previous ones did.

Knowing thus the cause of the pulse, it is easy to understand that it is not so much the blood contained in the veins of this machine, and which comes again from its liver, as that which is in its arteries, and which has already been distilled in its heart, which can attach itself to its other parts and serve to repair what their continual agitation and the various actions of the other bodies which surround them detach from them and cause to come out. 

For the blood which is in its veins always flows little by little from their extremities towards the heart (and the disposition of certain small doors, or valves, which the ana tomists have noticed in several places along our veins should persuade you enough that it happens in us all the same); but on the contrary that which is in its arteries is pushed out of the heart with effort, and with various small jolts, towards their extremities; so that it can easily go to join and 'unite with all its members, and thus maintain them, or even make them grow, if it represents the body of a man who is dis posed there.

For, as the arteries swell, the small parts of the blood which they contain go to choke here and there the roots of certain small threads which, coming out of the ends of the small branches of these arteries, compose the bones, the flesh, the skins, the nerves, the brain, and all the rest of the solid members, according to the various ways in which they join or intertwine; and so they have the power to push them a little in front of themselves and to put themselves in their place; then at the moment that the arteries unbend, each of its parts stops where it is, and by that alone is joined and united to those which it touches, according to what has been said above.

Now, if it is the body of a child that our model represents, its material will be so soft and its pores so large that it will be able to be seen by the observer. If it is the body of a child that our model represents, its matter will be so tender and its pores so easy to widen, that the parts of the blood which will thus enter into the composition of the rigid members, will commonly be a little larger than those in whose place they will be placed, or even it will happen that two or three will succeed together to one, which will be the cause of its growth: but nevertheless the material of its members will gradually harden, so that after a few years its pores will no longer be able to enlarge so much; and thus, ceasing to grow, it will represent the body of an older man.


Besides, there are only a few parts of the blood that can be united with the solid limbs in the way I have just explained; but most of it returns to the veins through the extremes of the arteries, which are in many places joined to those of the veins; But most of them return to the heart, and from there go back to the arteries, so that the movement of the blood in the body is only a perennial circulation.
Moreover, some of the parts of the blood go to the spleen and others to the gall bladder, and, both from the spleen and the gall bladder and immediately from the arteries, some return to the stomach and the intestines, where they serve as strong water to aid in the digestion of meats; And because they are carried from the heart almost in a moment by the arteries, they never fail to be very hot, so that their vapors can easily rise through the gullet to the mouth, where they combine with saliva. 

There are also some that flow in urine through the flesh of the kidneys, or in sweat and other excrements through the whole skin; and, in all these places, it is only the situation, or the figure, or the smallness of the pores through which they pass, that makes some of them not feel there rather than others, and that the rest of the blood cannot follow them, as you may have seen in various sieves which, being variously pierced, are used to separate various grains from one another. But the main thing to note here is that all the most lively, strongest and most subtle parts of this blood go into the concavities of the brain, inasmuch as the arteries that carry them there are those that come from the heart, the most straight of all, and that, as you know, all moving bodies tend, each as far as possible, to keep their movement in a straight line. 

See, for example, the heart, and consider that when the blood leaves it with effort through the opening B, there is no part of it that does not tend towards C, where the concavities of the brain are, but that the passage not being large enough to carry them all there, the weakest are diverted by the strongest, which by this means go there alone.


You may also notice, in passing, that beside those which enter the brain, there are none stronger or more lively than those which go to the vessels destined for the generation. For, for example, if those which have the strength to reach D cannot go further towards C, because there is not enough room for all of them, they turn rather towards E than towards F or G, inasmuch as the passage is straighter there. Then I could perhaps show you how, from the humor that gathers towards E, another machine similar to this one can be formed; but I do not want to go any further into this matter.

As for the parts of the blood which penetrate to the brain, they serve there not only to nourish and maintain its substance, but also mainly to produce a certain very subtle wind, or rather a very bright and pure flame, which we call animal spirits. For it must be known that the arteries which bring them from the heart, after having divided themselves into an infinite number of small branches, and having composed these small tissues which are spread like tapestries at the bottom of the concavities of the brain, gather around a certain small gland situated about the middle of the substance of this brain, and have at this point a great number of small holes through which the subtlest parts of the blood they contain can flow into this gland, but which are so narrow that they give no passage to the coarsest.

It is also necessary to know that these arteries do not stop there, but that, having assembled several in one, they go straight up and into this great vessel which is like a Euripus, with which the whole external surface of this brain is watered. And moreover it must be noticed that the larger parts of the blood can lose much of their agitation in the detours of the small tissues through which they pass, inasmuch as they have the strength to push the smaller ones which are among them, and thus to transfer it to them; but that these smaller ones cannot in the same way lose theirs, inasmuch as it is even increased by that which the larger ones transfer to them, and that there is no other body around them to which they can so easily transfer it. 

Hence it is easy to conceive that when the larger ones ascend straight to the outer surface of the brain, where they serve as food for its substance, they cause the smaller and more restless ones to turn away and all enter this gland, which must be imagined as a very abundant source, from which they flow at the same time on all sides into the cavities of the brain; And so, without any other preparation or change, except that they are separated from the coarser ones, and that they still retain the extreme speed that the heat of the heart has given them, they cease to have the form of blood, and are called animal spirits. 

Now, as these spirits thus enter the concavities of the brain, they pass from there into the pores of its substance, and from these pores into the nerves; where, according as they enter, or even only as they tend to enter more or less into the one than into the other, they have the power of changing the figure of the muscles into which its nerves are inserted, and by this means of making all the members move. As you may have seen in the caves and fountains in the gardens of our kings, the mere force with which the water moves as it emerges from its source is sufficient to move various machines, and even to make them play certain instruments, or pronounce certain words, according to the various arrangements of the pipes that control it.

The nerves of the human machine are like the pipes of the machines of these fountains, its muscles and its tendons to the other various devices and springs which serve to move them, its animal spirits to the water which moves them, of which the heart is the source, and of which the concavities of the brain are the eyes. 

Moreover, breathing and other such actions which are natural and ordinary to him, and which depend on the course of the spirits, are like the movements of a clock or a mill, which the ordinary course of water can make continuous. The external objects which, by their mere presence, act against the organs of her senses, and which by this means determine her to move in various ways, according to the disposition of the parts of her brain, are like strangers who, entering some of the caves of these fountains, cause themselves without thinking the movements which are made in their presence; for they can only enter by walking on certain tiles so arranged that, for example, if they approach a Diana who is bathing, they will hide her in the reeds; and if they pass beyond to follow her, they will make come towards them a Neptune, who will threaten them of his trident; And, finally, when the soul-seeking is in this machine, it will have its principal seat in the brain, and will be there like the cast iron, which must be in the eyes where all the pipes of these machines meet, when it wants to excite, or prevent, or change in some way their movements.

## The manufacture of nerves and muscles

It is through this manufacture alone that the spirits which are in the brain present themselves to enter into some nerves, they have the force to move at the same moment some member.

Then, having touched upon respiration, and such other simple and ordinary movements, I shall say how external objects act against the organs of the senses; and after that I shall explain in detail all that is done in the concavities and pores of the brain, how the animal spirits take their course there, and what are those of our functions which this machine can imitate by their means: For if I begin with the brain, and only follow the course of the spirits in order, as I have done that of the blood, it seems to me that my discourse could not be at all so clear.

Finally, according to the other various ways in which they are moved, they will make it feel all the feelings of the body. Although they are very loose and easy to move, they are not so loose that they can carry to the brain all the smallest actions that are in nature, but that the least that they bring to it are those of the coarsest parts of the earthly bodies; and even that there may be some of these bodies whose parts, though large enough, will not let themselves slip against these little nets so gently that they will press or cut them off completely, without their action passing to the brain; just as there are certain drugs which have the power to dull or even corrupt those of our limbs against which they are applied, without making us have any feeling.
