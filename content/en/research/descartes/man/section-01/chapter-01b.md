The pulse, or beat of the arteries, depends on the 11 little skins. These are like little doors which close and open the entrances of the 4 vessels which look into the 2 cavities of the heart.

When one of these beats ceases, another is about to begin. At that moment:
- the little doors which are at the entrances of the 2 arteries are exactly closed
- those which are at the entrances of the 2 veins are opened

In this way, 2 drops of blood fall through these 2 veins, one in each cavity of the heart. 

These drops of blood then become rarefied, and suddenly spread out into a larger space without any reason than the one they occupied before, pushing and closing those little doors which are at the entrances of the 2 veins.

This prevents any more blood from going down into the heart, and pushing and opening those of the two arteries, through which they enter promptly and with effort, thus making the heart and all the arteries of the body swell at the same time.

But immediately afterwards, this rarefied blood condenses again, or enters the other parts; and so the heart and arteries swell up, the little doors at the two entrances of the arteries close again, and those at the entrances of the two veins open again, and give passage to two other drops of blood, which again swell up the heart and arteries, just as the previous ones did.

It is the blood distilled in the heart that can attach itself to its other parts and repair what their continual agitation and the actions of the other bodies which surround them detach from them and cause to come out. 

For the blood which is in its veins always flows little by little from their extremities towards the heart (and the disposition of certain small doors, or valves, which the ana tomists have noticed in several places along our veins should persuade you enough that it happens in us all the same); 

but on the contrary that which is in its arteries is pushed out of the heart with effort, and with various small jolts, towards their extremities; so that it can easily go to join and 'unite with all its members, and thus maintain them, or even make them grow, if it represents the body of a man who is dis posed there.

As the arteries swell, the small parts of the blood which they contain go to choke here and there the roots of certain small threads which, coming out of the ends of the small branches of these arteries, compose the bones, the flesh, the skins, the nerves, the brain, and all the rest of the solid members, according to the various ways in which they join or intertwine.

And so they have the power to push them a little in front of themselves and to put themselves in their place; then at the moment that the arteries unbend, each of its parts stops where it is, and by that alone is joined and united to those which it touches, according to what has been said above.

If it is the body of a child that our model represents, its material will be so soft and its pores so large that it will be able to be seen by the observer. 

If it is the body of a child that our model represents, its matter will be so tender and its pores so easy to widen, that the parts of the blood which will thus enter into the composition of the rigid members, will commonly be a little larger than those in whose place they will be placed, or even it will happen that two or three will succeed together to one, which will be the cause of its growth: 

But nevertheless the material of its members will gradually harden, so that after a few years its pores will no longer be able to enlarge so much; and thus, ceasing to grow, it will represent the body of an older man.

Besides, there are only a few parts of the blood that can be united with the solid limbs in the way I have just explained; but most of it returns to the veins through the extremes of the arteries, which are in many places joined to those of the veins; But most of them return to the heart, and from there go back to the arteries, so that the movement of the blood in the body is only a perennial circulation.

Moreover, some of the parts of the blood go to the spleen and others to the gall bladder, and, both from the spleen and the gall bladder and immediately from the arteries, some return to the stomach and the intestines, where they serve as strong water to aid in the digestion of meats. 

Because they are carried from the heart almost in a moment by the arteries, they never fail to be very hot, so that their vapors can easily rise through the gullet to the mouth, where they combine with saliva. 

There are also some that flow in urine through the flesh of the kidneys, or in sweat and other excrements through the whole skin; and, in all these places, it is only the situation, or the figure, or the smallness of the pores through which they pass, that makes some of them not feel there rather than others, and that the rest of the blood cannot follow them, as you may have seen in various sieves which, being variously pierced, are used to separate various grains from one another. 

All the most lively, strongest and most subtle parts of this blood go into the cavities of the brain.

The arteries that connect the heart to the brain are the straightest of all. 

This is because all moving bodies keep their movement in a straight line as much as possible. 

See, for example, the heart, and consider that when the blood leaves it with effort through the opening B, there is no part of it that does not tend towards C, where the concavities of the brain are, but that the passage not being large enough to carry them all there, the weakest are diverted by the strongest, which by this means go there alone.


Besides those which enter the brain, there are none stronger or more lively than those which go to the vessels destined for the generation. 

For, for example, if those which have the strength to reach D cannot go further towards C, because there is not enough room for all of them, they turn rather towards E than towards F or G, inasmuch as the passage is straighter there. Then 

I could perhaps show you how, from the humor that gathers towards E, another machine similar to this one can be formed; but I do not want to go any further into this matter.

As for the parts of the blood which penetrate to the brain, they serve there not only to nourish and maintain its substance, but also mainly to produce a certain very subtle wind, or rather a very bright and pure flame, which we call animal spirits. 

The arteries which bring them from the heart, after having divided themselves into an infinite number of small branches, and having composed these small tissues which are spread like tapestries at the bottom of the concavities of the brain, gather around a certain small gland situated about the middle of the substance of this brain, and have at this point a great number of small holes through which the subtlest parts of the blood they contain can flow into this gland, but which are so narrow that they give no passage to the coarsest.

These arteries do not stop there. Several of them assemble as one. They go straight up and into this great vessel which is like a Euripus, with which the whole external surface of this brain is watered. 

The larger parts of the blood can lose much of their agitation in the detours of the small tissues through which they pass, inasmuch as they have the strength to push the smaller ones which are among them, and thus to transfer it to them.

But these smaller ones cannot in the same way lose theirs, inasmuch as it is even increased by that which the larger ones transfer to them, and that there is no other body around them to which they can so easily transfer it. 

Hence, when the larger ones ascend straight to the outer surface of the brain, where they serve as food for its substance, they cause the smaller and more restless ones to turn away and all enter this gland, which must be imagined as a very abundant source, from which they flow at the same time on all sides into the cavities of the brain. 

And so, without any other preparation or change, except that they are separated from the coarser ones, and that they still retain the extreme speed that the heat of the heart has given them, they cease to have the form of blood, and are called animal spirits. 


These spirits thus enter the cavities of the brain. From there, they pass into the pores of its substance. From these pores, they enter into the nerves.

As they enter the nerves, they have the power to change the shape of the muscles they are connected to. This makes all the members move.
