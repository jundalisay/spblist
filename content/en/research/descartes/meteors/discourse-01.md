We naturally have more admiration for things above us than for those at the same height or below. 

The clouds scarcely exceed the summits of some mountains, and we often see some lower than the tops of our church spires, nevertheless, because we must look up to the sky to see them, we imagine them to be so elevated that even poets and painters compose the throne of God from them and depict Him using His own hands to open and close the doors of the winds, to pour dew on the flowers, and to hurl lightning at the rocks. 

This leads me to hope that if I explain their nature here in such a way that one no longer has occasion to admire anything seen in them or that descends from them, it will be easily believed that it is possible in the same way to find the causes of everything most admirable on Earth.

I will speak in this first discourse of the nature of terrestrial bodies in general, so that I can better explain in the following one the nature of exhalations and vapors.

Then, because these vapors rising from the seawater sometimes form salt above its surface, I will take that occasion to describe it a little and to see if we can understand the forms of these bodies that philosophers say are composed of elements by a perfect mixture, as well as those of meteors, which they say are composed of elements by an imperfect mixture. 

After that, by guiding the vapors through the air, I will examine where the winds come from; and by gathering them in some places, I will describe the nature of clouds. By dissolving these clouds, I will explain what causes rain, hail, and snow; I will not forget the snow whose parts have the shape of small six-pointed stars very perfectly arranged, which, although not observed by the ancients, is nonetheless one of Nature’s rarest wonders. I will also not forget tempests, thunder, lightning, and the various fires that ignite in the air, or the lights that are seen there. 

But above all, I will endeavor to accurately depict the rainbow and explain the reason for its colors, in such a way that one can also understand the nature of all those found in other subjects. To this, I will add the cause of those commonly seen in the clouds and of the halos that surround the stars; and finally, the cause of the multiple suns or moons that sometimes appear together.

The knowledge of these things depends on the general principles of Nature, which have not yet been, as far as I know, well explained. Therefore, I will have to start with some suppositions, as I did in the Dioptrics. But I will strive to make them so simple and easy that you may not have difficulty believing them, even though I have not demonstrated them.


Water, earth, air, and all other such bodies that surround us are composed of many small parts of various shapes and sizes, which are never so well arranged nor so tightly joined together that there do not remain many gaps around them. 

These gaps are not empty, but filled with this very subtle matter, through the mediation of which I have said above that the action of light is communicated. Then, in particular, I suppose that the small parts that water is composed of are long, smooth, and slippery, like little eels, which, although they join and intertwine, never tie or hook together in such a way that they cannot be easily separated. 

Conversely, almost all those of earth as well as of air, and most other bodies, have very irregular and uneven shapes; so that they cannot be even slightly intertwined without catching and binding to one another, just as the various branches of shrubs that grow together in a hedge do. And when they bind in this way, they form hard bodies, like earth, wood, or similar substances. 

Whereas, if they are simply placed one on top of the other, without being very much or at all intertwined, and if they are also so small that they can be moved and separated by the agitation of the subtle matter surrounding them, they must occupy a lot of space and form liquid bodies, very rarefied and very light, like oils or air. 

The subtle matter fills the gaps between the parts of these bodies. It never ceases to move back and forth very quickly, not exactly at the same speed everywhere and at all times, but that it commonly moves a little faster near the surface of the earth than it does at the height of the air where the clouds are, and faster near the equator than near the poles, and in the same place faster in summer than in winter, and faster during the day than at night. 

The reason for this is evident, assuming that light is nothing other than a certain movement or action by which luminous bodies push this subtle matter in all directions around them in a straight line, as was said in the Dioptrics. 

For it follows from this that the rays of the sun, both direct and reflected, must agitate it more during the day than at night, more in summer than in winter, and more near the equator than near the poles, and more against the earth than towards the clouds. 

It follows that this subtle matter is composed of various parts, which, although they are all very small, are nonetheless much less so than the others, and that the largest, or rather the least small, always have the most force, just as generally all large bodies have more force than the smaller ones when they are equally shaken. 

This means that the less subtle this matter is, that is, composed of less small parts, the more it can agitate the parts of other bodies. And this also makes it so that it is usually the least subtle in places and at times where it is most agitated, such as near the surface of the earth rather than towards the clouds, near the equator rather than near the poles, in summer rather than in winter, and during the day rather than at night. The reason is that the larger parts, having more force, can better move towards places where the agitation is greater, making it easier for them to continue their movement. Nevertheless, there are always many very small parts that slip among these larger ones. 

All terrestrial bodies have many pores through which these smaller parts can pass, but that there are several which have pores so narrow or arranged in such a way that they do not accept the larger parts; and that it is usually these that feel the coldest when touched or even when approached. 

Thus, because marble and metals feel colder than wood, one must think that their pores do not easily accept the less subtle parts of this matter; and that the pores of ice accept them even less easily than those of marble or metals, because it is even colder. For I suppose here that for cold and heat, there is no need to conceive anything other than that the small parts of the bodies we touch, being agitated more or less strongly than usual, either by the small parts of this subtle matter or by any other cause, also agitate more or less the small filaments of those of our nerves that are the organs of touch. 

When they agitate them more strongly than usual, it causes in us the sensation of heat; whereas when they agitate them less strongly, it causes the sensation of cold. 

Although this subtle matter does not separate the parts of hard bodies, which are like intertwined branches, in the same way that it does those of water and all other bodies that are liquid; it nonetheless agitates and makes them tremble more or less, according to how strong its movement is and how large its parts are. Just as the wind can agitate all the branches of shrubs that form a hedge without removing them from their places.

There is such a proportion between the force of this subtle matter and the resistance of the parts of other bodies, that when it is as agitated and not more subtle than it usually is in these regions near the earth, it has the force to agitate and move separately one from another, and even to bend most of the small parts of the water among which it slips, thus making it liquid. 

But when it is not more agitated, nor less subtle, than it usually is in these regions at the height of the air, or as it sometimes is in winter against the earth, it does not have enough force to bend and agitate them in this way, which causes them to remain confusedly joined and laid one on top of the other, thus composing a hard body, namely ice. 

So that you can imagine the same difference between water and ice as you would between a pile of small eels, whether alive or dead, floating in a fisherman's boat full of holes through which the river water passes and agitates them, and a pile of the same eels all dry and stiff with cold on the shore. 

And because water never freezes unless the matter between its parts is more subtle than usual, it follows that the pores of the ice that form then, being accommodated only to the size of the more subtle parts of this matter, are arranged in such a way that they cannot accept those that are less subtle; and thus the ice is always very cold, even when kept until summer; and even then it retains its hardness without gradually softening like wax, because the heat only penetrates inside as the surface becomes liquid.
