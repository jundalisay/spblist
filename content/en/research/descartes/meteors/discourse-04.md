Any perceptible movement of air is called wind, and any invisible and intangible substance is called air. Thus, when water is greatly rarefied and changed into very subtle vapor, it is said to be converted into air, even though this great air that we breathe is, for the most part, composed of particles that have shapes very different from those of water, and are much finer.

Therefore, air being expelled from a bellows or propelled by a fan is called wind; although these broader winds, which prevail over the face of the sea and land, are usually nothing more than the movement of vapors that, in expanding, pass from where they are to somewhere else where they find more room to spread out. 

Similarly, as seen in these spheres called Aeolipiles, a little water vaporizing into steam creates a wind strong enough and large enough because of the tiny matter that it comprises. And because this artificial wind can greatly help us understand these natural ones, it will be good to explain it here.

The text describes an Æolipile, an early steam turbine device, and explains how it works. Here's the translation into English:

"ABCDE is a hollow sphere made of copper or similar material, completely closed except for a very small opening marked D. The part of this sphere ABC is filled with water, and the other part AEC is empty, containing only air. It is placed over fire; the heat causes the water molecules to move, with many rising above the surface AC, where they spread out, swirl around, and push against each other, attempting to move apart as described above. Because they cannot move apart freely, as some escape through the hole D, all the forces causing them to push against each other combine to push out those closest to the hole, thus creating a wind that blows towards F. 

Since new parts of this water are constantly being heated and rising above the surface AC, spreading and moving apart from each other as they exit through the hole D, this wind continues until all the water in the sphere is evaporated, or until the heat causing the evaporation ceases. 

Now, the ordinary winds that prevail in the air form in much the same way as this one, with mainly two differences. The first is that the vapors they are composed of do not just rise from the surface of water, as in this sphere, but also from moist earth, snow, and clouds. Therefore, they usually emerge in greater abundance than from pure water, because their particles are already mostly separated and disjointed, and thus easier to separate further. 

The second difference is that these vapors, unlike those in an Æolipile, cannot be contained in the air; rather, they are hindered from spreading evenly in all directions by the resistance of other vapors, clouds, mountains, or by winds blowing towards their location. However, elsewhere in the atmosphere, other vapors often thicken and contract at the same time the former expand, directing them towards the space left by the latter.

For example, imagine that there are currently numerous vapors at the point in the air marked F, expanding and tending to occupy a much larger space than they currently occupy; simultaneously, there are others at G, condensing and converting into water or snow, leaving most of the space they previously occupied."


You would not doubt that those vapors towards F will move towards G, thus forming a wind that blows in that direction. Especially if you consider that they are hindered from spreading towards A and B by high mountains there, and towards E because the air is pressurized and condensed by another wind blowing from C to D. Moreover, there are clouds above them that prevent them from spreading higher into the sky. 

Notice that when vapors move in this way from one place to another, they carry or push ahead all the air they encounter on their path, and all the exhalations along with it. Thus, although they almost single-handedly cause the winds, they do not solely constitute them. Also, the expansion and condensation of these exhalations and air can contribute to the production of these winds, but this contribution is minimal compared to the expansion and condensation of vapors. 

Air, when expanded, occupies only about two or three times more space than when moderately condensed, whereas vapors occupy more than two or three thousand times more space. Exhalations do not expand, meaning they do not separate from terrestrial bodies, except with great heat; they can hardly ever be as condensed again by cold as they were before. In contrast, very little heat is needed to expand water into vapor, and very little cold to turn vapors back into water.


But let's now consider in detail the properties and generation of the principal winds. Firstly, it is observed that all the air has its course around the Earth from East to West. This we must assume here, because the reason for it cannot easily be deduced without explaining the entire structure of the universe, which is not my intention here. However, it is further observed that the eastern winds are usually much drier and make the air clearer and more serene than the western winds. The reason for this is that the western winds, opposing the usual course of vapors, trap them and cause them to thicken into clouds, whereas the eastern winds push them away and disperse them.

Additionally, it is observed that it is mainly in the morning that the eastern winds blow, and in the evening that the western winds blow. The reason for this will be clear if you consider the Earth ABCD and the Sun S, which, by illuminating the half ABC and casting noon towards B and midnight towards D, sets simultaneously in respect to the peoples who dwell towards A and rises in respect to those who are towards C. Because the vapors near B are greatly expanded by the heat of the day, they flow partly towards A and partly towards C, to D, where they occupy the space left by those condensed by the coolness of the night. Thus, they create a west wind towards A, where the sun sets, and an east wind towards C, where it rises. It is also worth noting that this wind towards C is usually stronger and faster than the one towards A, both because it follows the course of the entire mass of air and because the part of the Earth between C and D, having been without sunlight for a longer time than the part between D and A, has had more time and greater condensation of vapors.

It is also observed that it is mainly during the day that the north winds blow, coming from high to low, and they are very violent, cold, and dry. You can see the reason for this by considering that the Earth EBFD is covered with several clouds and fogs towards the poles E and F, where it is hardly warmed by the sun, and towards B, where it shines directly, it stirs up a quantity of vapors. These vapors, being strongly agitated by the action of its light, rise very quickly until they are so elevated that the resistance of their weight makes it easier for them to turn aside and take their course to both sides towards I and M above the clouds G and K, rather than continuing straight upward. And these clouds G and K, being also heated and rarified by the sun at the same time, turn into vapors that flow from G to H and from K to L rather than towards E and F, because the dense air towards the poles resists them much more than the vapors rising from the Earth towards the south, which, being very agitated and ready to move in all directions, can easily yield their place.



The passage describes the nature of winds in relation to the Earth's atmosphere and geographical zones. Here is a translation into English:

"Thus, taking F for the Arctic Pole, the course of these vapors from K towards L creates a north wind, which blows during the day in Europe. And this wind blows downwards because it comes from the clouds towards the earth. It is usually very strong because it is stirred by the strongest heat of all, namely that of midday; and from the easiest material to dissolve into vapor, namely from the clouds. Finally, this wind is very cold and very dry, partly because of its strength, following what was said above that impetuous winds are always dry and cold; It is also dry because it is usually composed only of the coarser parts of fresh water mixed with air, whereas humidity mainly depends on the finer parts; and these are hardly found in the clouds from which it originates; for, as you will soon see, they partake much more of the nature of ice than of water. And it is cold because it brings with it towards the south the very subtle matter that was towards the north, on which primarily the coldness depends. On the contrary, it is observed that the southern winds blow more often during the night, coming from below upwards, and are gentle and humid. The reason for this can also be seen by looking again at the earth EBFD, and considering that its part D, which is under the Equator and where I suppose it is now night, still retains enough heat that the sun has communicated to it during the day to release several vapors; but the air above towards P does not retain as much in proportion. For generally, coarse and heavy bodies always retain their heat longer than those that are light and subtle; and those that are hard also retain it longer than those that are liquid. This is why the vapors found towards P, instead of continuing their course towards Q and R, stop and thicken into clouds, which, by preventing those coming from the earth D from rising higher, force them to take their course to the sides towards N and O, thus creating a southern wind, which blows mainly during the night and comes from below upwards, namely from the earth towards the air; and it can only be very slow, both because its course is delayed by the density of the night air, and also because its material, coming only from the earth or water, cannot expand so rapidly or in such quantity as that of other winds, which usually come from clouds. And finally, it is warm and humid, partly because of the slowness of its course; and it is humid because it is composed of the finest parts of fresh water as well as the coarsest, for they come out together from the earth; and it is warm because it brings with it towards the north the subtle matter that was towards the south. It is also observed that in the month of March, and generally throughout spring, the winds are drier, and the changes of weather more sudden and frequent, than in any other season of the year. The reason for this is also seen by looking at the earth EBFD again and thinking that the sun, which I suppose is opposite the circle BAD representing the Equator, and was three months earlier opposite the circle HN representing the Tropic of Capricorn, has heated much less the half of the earth BFD where it is now spring, than the other half BED where it is autumn; and consequently, this half BFD is much more covered with snow, and all the air surrounding it is much denser and filled with more clouds than that surrounding the other half BED; which causes during the day much more vapor to expand there, and conversely much more to condense during the night. As the mass of the earth there is less heated and the force of the sun there is not less, there must be more inequality between the day's heat and the night's cold: and so these eastern winds, which I said blow mainly in the morning, and those from the north, which blow around midday, both of which are very dry, must there be much stronger and more abundant than in any other season. And because the western winds, which blow in the evening, must also be quite strong there, for the same reason as the eastern ones that blow in the morning; provided the regular course of these winds is not advanced, or delayed, or diverted by the particular causes that can more or less expand or thicken the air in each region, they encounter each other and generate rains or storms, which usually cease soon after because the eastern and northern winds, which drive away the clouds, remain dominant. And I believe that these eastern and northern winds are what the Greeks called Ornithies, because they bring back the birds that come in spring. But as for the Etesians, which they observed after the summer solstice, it is quite true that they proceed from the vapors that the sun raises from the lands and waters of the North, after having already spent quite a long time towards the Tropic of Cancer. For you know that it lingers much more proportionally towards the Tropics than it does in the space between the two; and it must be thought that during the months of March, April, and May, it dissolves into vapors and winds most of the clouds and snows that are towards our Pole; but it cannot heat the lands and waters enough there to raise other vapors that cause winds until a few weeks later, when this great day of six months, which it has there, is a little beyond its midday."
