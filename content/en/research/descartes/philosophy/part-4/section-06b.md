## {{< s v="4.152" >}} When the iron particles are moved through the same channels as the magnet, they flow in the same way as the earth. Why?

<!-- Why does one magnet convert and incline another, just as it does with the earth?  -->

Two spherical magnets convert and incline each other. So too should they convert and incline the whole earth. 

There are always more particles around each magnet than in the surrounding air. 

This is because the magnets have channels through which the particles flow much more easily than through the surrounding air. This retains them  near the magnet. 

Moreover, due to the channels that are found within the earth, there are more of these particles in the entire air, and also in the bodies of the earth that surround it, than there is in the sky.

The power of magnetism is the same in all things, whether in a magnet or in the earth, which is the greatest magnet of all.

## {{< s v="4.153" >}} Why do two magnets attract each other? What is the nature of their interaction?

It is not true that two magnets simply turn towards each other until the northern pole of one magnet faces the southern pole of the other, and vice versa. 

Rather, after they have turned towards each other, they approach each other until they touch. They stick together as long as nothing prevents their motion. 

The iron particles are moved with great speed when they are in the magnetic field because they are carried by the fire-aether which is the cause of their motion.

They are not slowed down by the air-aether nor the earth-aether which are not as fertile.

This is why the particles that pass through the magnet `1`, with the speed at which they move from `S` to `N`, acquire the power to move further along the straight lines `R` and `C`.

This is until they encounter many particles of the air-aether or earth-aether, which reflect them back towards `V`. 

They move in this way in the entire space `RVC`. This space is called the sphere of activity of the magnet `1`.

The larger the magnet, the more its activity. The longer the fertile line `SN`, the greater the agitation of the particles that move through it.

Similarly, the particles that pass through the magnet `2`, move in a straight line in both directions. They are reflected back towards `X`. They propel the entire air in the sphere of activity.

But they do not have a place to retreat to, since they are not reflected back towards `V` and `R`. Instead, they move towards Magnet `2`, and then they are reflected back towards Magnet `1`. 

They move towards Magnet `2`, and are reflected back towards Magnet `1`, and so on.

Therefore, the fire-aether particles move in the same way through two Magnets `1` and `2` and in a single magnet. 

It is easier for the fire-aether particles to move:
- from Magnet `1` to Magnet `2`, and from Magnet `2` to Magnet `1`, than to be reflected back towards `V` and `R`. 
- from Magnet `2` to `C`, and from `C` to Magnet `2`, than to be reflected back towards `X`.

And so, these fire-aether particles <!-- of iron --> move in a straight line through these two magnets `1` and `2`, until they touch. And then they stick together, as long as nothing prevents their motion.

{{< s v="4.154" >}} **Why do magnets sometimes repel each other when they are brought close together?**

<!-- However, the poles of the two magnets do not approach each other in that way; rather, if they are brought too close, they actually repel each other.  -->

The fire-aether particles that come from one magnet's pole and move towards the other magnet's pole cannot enter the other magnet's pole. Instead, they move around it. 

This creates a space between the two magnets. 

However, when the distance between the two magnets is increased, the fire-aether particles move in a curved path towards the other magnet's pole. These then bounce back towards the first magnet's pole. 

This motion is not a simple rotation, but rather a complex motion that involves the interaction of the particles with the magnetic field.

The fire-aether particles coming out of Magnet `1` through the pole `S` cannot enter into Magnet `2` through its pole `s`. They need some space between `S` and `s`, through which to pass towards `V` and `X`. This force moves them from `N` to `S`, repelling Magnet `2`.

And so, coming out of Magnet `2` they repel the Magnet `1` at least when their axes `NS` and `ns` are in the same straight line. 

But when they are bent ever so little more to one side than to the other, then these magnets turn themselves, in the manner explained before. If  this turning is prevented, but not their straight motion, then again one magnet drives the other away in a straight line.

Assume that:
- Magnet `2` is placed on a small boat with its axis upright perpendicular to the surface
- Magnet `1` is placed near, with its North pole facing the North pole of Magnet `2`

If Magnet `1` is moved towards `Y`, Magnet 2 will move away towards `Z` before it touches Magnet `1`.

This is because no matter how the boat turns, there always needs to be some space between these two magnets for the striped particles exiting through poles N and n to pass through towards V and X.
