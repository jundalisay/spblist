### 38. According to Tycho's hypothesis, the Earth must move around its own center

The Brahean hypothesis is now commonly accepted by all those who reject the Copernican.

<!-- To Brahe, the Earth has more motion than to Copernicus. -->

Firstly, with the Earth remaining immobile according to their opinion, it is necessary that the entire heaven, along with the stars, revolves around it every day; which cannot be understood without simultaneously understanding the translation of all parts of the Earth from the vicinity of the parts of the heavens they touch to the vicinity of others. 

Since this translation is reciprocal, as previously mentioned, and the same force or action is required for it on Earth as in the heavens, there is no reason why we should attribute the motion to the heavens rather than to the Earth because of it; 

It must be attributed to the Earth only; because it occurs over its entire surface, not in the same way over the entire surface of the heavens, but only over the concave part contiguous to the Earth, which is very small compared to the convex. 

Nor does it matter if they say they believe not only the concave surface of the starry heaven is separated from the Earth but also the convex from another heaven surrounding it, namely the crystalline or Empyrean heaven; 

This is why they attribute that motion to the heavens rather than to the Earth. 

For no argument can be made to prove such a separation of the entire convex surface of the starry heaven from another heaven surrounding it; 

But they invent it purely arbitrarily. 

Thus, according to their hypothesis, the reason why the motion must be attributed to the Earth is certain and evident; 

But the reason why they attribute it to the heavens and quiet to the Earth is uncertain and imagined solely by them.


### 39. It moves around the Sun with annual motion.

From the same Tycho's hypothesis, the Sun, revolving annually around the Earth, not only carries Mercury and Venus but also Mars, Jupiter, and Saturn, which are farther from it than the Earth is. 

This cannot be understood, especially in a fluid heaven, such as they suppose, without the entire interposing matter of the heavens being carried along simultaneously.

Meanwhile, the Earth being separated by some force from the parts of this matter contiguous to it and describing a circle within it. 

Therefore, this separation again, which involves the entire Earth and requires a peculiar action on it, must be called its motion.


### 40. The translation of the Earth does not cause any change in the appearance of the Fixed Stars because of their immense distance

However, one problem remains in my hypothesis: if the Sun always maintains the same position among the Fixed Stars, it is necessary that the Earth, which moves around it, approaches and recedes from them by the entire distance of its orbit, which has not yet been observed from the phenomena. 

But this is excused by the immense distance that we suppose to exist between us and the fixed stars; namely, such that the entire circle described by the Earth around the Sun, if compared to it, must be considered as a point. 

This may seem incredible to those not accustomed to considering the wonders of God and who view the Earth as the principal part of the universe and the dwelling place of man, for whom all other things were made. 

But for Astronomers, who all now know that it is like a point compared to the heavens, it should not seem so strange.


### 41. This great distance of the fixed stars is also required for the motions of Comets
<!-- , which are now known to be in the heavens -->

Comets are now well established not to move in our atmosphere. They <!-- , as the excessively primitive opinion once held,  --> require this vast space between the sphere of Saturn and the fixed stars to complete all their excursions. For their motions are so varied, so vast, and so discrepant from the stability of the fixed stars and the regular revolutions of the planets around the Sun, that without this distance, they seem not to be reconcilable with any laws of nature. 

Nor should it concern us that Tycho and other astronomers, who diligently investigated their parallaxes, stated that they are only above the Moon, towards the sphere of Venus or Mercury, but not above Saturn itself. 

For they could have concluded this no less correctly from their calculations than the other; but when disputing against the ancients, who counted comets among sublunary meteors, they were content to show that they are in the heavens and did not dare to attribute all the height they calculated to them, lest it should be less easily believed.
