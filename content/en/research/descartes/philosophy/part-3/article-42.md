### 42. All things we see here on Earth also pertain to phenomena, but it is not necessary to initially consider everything.

Besides these more general phenomena, many particulars could also be enumerated here, not only concerning the Sun, planets, comets, and fixed stars, but especially also concerning the Earth (namely, all those things we see on its surface) among the phenomena here.

For to recognize the true nature of this visible world, it is not enough to find some causes by which those things we observe from afar in the heavens can be explained, but from the same causes, all those things we observe up close on Earth must also be deduced.

Yet, it is not necessary to consider all those things to determine the causes of more general matters; but we will then recognize that we have correctly determined those causes when we find that from the same causes, not only those things we considered but also all other things we had not previously thought of can be explained.


### 43. It is hardly possible that the causes from which all phenomena are clearly deduced are not true.

If we use no principles other than those most evidently perceived, if we deduce nothing from them except through mathematical consequences, and in the meantime, if those things which we thus deduce from them accurately agree with all the phenomena of nature, we would seem to do an injustice to God if we suspected that the causes of things thus discovered by us were false, as if He had created us so imperfect that we are deceived by the right use of our reason.


### 44. Those which I will present here to be considered only as hypotheses.

However, lest we seem too arrogant if, by philosophizing about such great matters, we affirm that we have discovered their true nature, I would prefer to leave this in doubt and propose all that I will write henceforth as a hypothesis; which, although it may be considered false, I will think I have done a sufficiently great service if all that is deduced from it agrees with experiments.

For in this way, we will derive as much usefulness for life from it as from the knowledge of the truth itself.


### 45. I will even assume here some things known to be false.

To better explain natural things, I will here trace their causes deeper than I believe they ever actually existed. For there is no doubt that the world was created from the beginning with all its perfection, so that in it both the Sun and Earth, and the Moon and stars existed; and also on Earth, not only the seeds of plants but the plants themselves; and Adam and Eve were not born as infants but were created as adults. Christian faith teaches us this; and natural reason also clearly persuades it. 

For, considering the immense power of God, we cannot think that He ever made anything that was not complete in all its parts. Nevertheless, to understand the natures of plants or humans, it is much better to consider how they could gradually arise from seeds than how they were created by God at the beginning of the world.

Thus, if we can devise principles, very simple and easy to understand, from which, as from certain seeds, we can demonstrate that both the stars and the Earth, and finally everything that we observe in this visible world, could have arisen, even though we know well they never did so arise, we will still better explain their nature than if we merely described them as they are now. And since I think I have found such principles, I will briefly explain them here.


### 46. What I assume here to explain all phenomena

All the bodies in the world are composed of one and the same matter, divisible into any parts, and already in fact divided into many, which move in various ways and have some sort of circular motions, and always maintain the same quantity of motion in the universe. 

But how large these parts of matter are, how quickly they move, and what kind of circles they describe, we cannot determine by reason alone; because they could have been arranged by God in countless different ways, and which one He chose above the others, only experience can teach us. Now, therefore, we are free to assume anything about them, provided all that follows from it agrees with experience. 

So, if you please, we will suppose that all that matter from which this visible world is composed was at the beginning divided by God into particles almost equal to each other, of moderate size, or median between all those from which the heavens and stars are now composed, and that all of them had as much motion in them as is now found in the world; and that they were moved equally, both individually around their own centers, and separately from each other, so that they composed a fluid body, such as we think the heavens are; and also several together around other points equally distant from each other and arranged in the same way as now, the centers of the fixed stars; and also around other somewhat more numerous points that match the number of planets. 

Thus, all those contained in the space AEINB (see figure on the next page) would turn around point S, and those contained in the space AEV around F, and so on for the others: thus forming as many different vortices as there are stars in the world.
