### 30. All planets revolve around the Sun. 

The motion of the Earth is similar to the motion of the other planets.

The Earth and the other planets are carried along by a common vortex, with the Sun at its center  and move in the motion of the vortex.

If obstructions are placed in the water where rivers flow, the water forms a vortex and carries along with it the obstructions.
- Some of them even turn around their own centers and perform a complete rotation faster than the center of the vortex.

The motion of the planets is always circular. 
- But it is rare for them to describe perfect circles.
- Instead, they deviate slightly in longitude and latitude.

<!-- ### 28. The Earth, properly speaking, does not move, nor do any of the planets, even though they are carried along by the heavens. 

Here we must remember what was said above about the nature of motion; namely, that (if we speak properly and according to the truth of the matter) it is merely the translation of one body from the vicinity of those bodies which immediately touch it and are regarded as stationary, into the vicinity of others. 

But often, according to common usage, any action by which a body migrates from one place to another is called motion; and in this sense, the same thing can be said to move and not move at the same time, depending on how we define its place. 

Hence, it follows that no proper motion can be found in the Earth, nor in the other planets, because they are not transferred from the vicinity of the parts of the heavens which immediately touch them, insofar as those parts of the heavens are considered immobile. 

For this, they should be entirely separated from all of them at once, which does not happen; but because the matter of the heavens is fluid, sometimes one of its particles, sometimes another, is removed from the planet it touches.

This happens through motion which should be attributed to those particles alone, and not to the planet: just as the partial translations of water and air that take place on the Earth's surface are usually attributed not to the Earth itself, but to those parts of water and air that are transferred.


### 29. No motion should be attributed to the Earth, even if motion is improperly understood according to common usage; but then it is correct to say that other planets move. 

However, taking motion according to common usage, it must be said that all the other planets, as well as the Sun and fixed stars, move; but it can be said very incongruously of the Earth. 

For the common people determine the positions of the stars by considering the parts of the Earth as immobile; and they judge the stars to move insofar as they recede from such determined places: which is convenient for the purposes of life, and therefore reasonable.

All of us from childhood have thought that the Earth is not globular, but flat, and that everywhere on it the same up and down, the same world directions—East, West, South, and North—exist; which we have therefore used to designate the positions of all other bodies. 

But if any philosopher, noticing that the Earth is a globe contained in a fluid and mobile heaven, and that the Sun and fixed stars always keep the same position among themselves, were to use them as immobile to determine its position, and therefore affirm it to move, he would speak without reason. 

For first, according to the philosophical sense, place should not be determined by very distant bodies, such as the fixed stars, but by those contiguous to that which is said to move. 

According to common usage, there is no reason to consider the fixed stars immobile rather than the Earth, except that he thinks there are no other bodies beyond them from which they could be separated and in respect to which they could be said to move, while the Earth remains at rest in the sense that he says the Earth moves in respect to the fixed stars. 

But thinking this is unreasonable; for since our mind is such that it recognizes no limits in the world, whoever considers the immensity of God and the weakness of our senses will judge it more reasonable to suspect that beyond all those fixed stars we see, there are perhaps other bodies, in comparison with which the Earth can be said to rest, and all those stars together to move, than to suspect that no such bodies can exist.


### 30. All the planets are carried around the Sun by the heavens. 

Thus, having removed all doubt about the Earth's motion, let us think that the whole matter of the heavens in which the planets move, revolves continuously in the manner of a vortex, with the Sun at its center, and that its parts closer to the Sun move more quickly than those further away, and that all the planets (of which the Earth is one) always move among these parts of the celestial matter. 

From this alone, without any machinery, all their phenomena are most easily understood. For just as in those places of rivers where the water forms a vortex turning upon itself, if various straws float on that water, we will see them carried along with it, and some even rotating around their own centers, completing their entire circuit the faster the closer they are to the center of the vortex; and finally, although they always tend to circular motions, they rarely describe completely perfect circles, but deviate somewhat in length and breadth. Thus, we can easily imagine the same for the planets, and by this alone all their phenomena are explained." -->
