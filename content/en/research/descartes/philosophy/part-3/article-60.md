### 61. It makes the bodies of the Sun and fixed stars round.

All the globules that revolve around `S` in the vortex `AEI` try to move away from `S`.

Those in the straight line `SA` all press each other towards `A`.

Those in the straight line `SE` press each other towards E, and so on for the others. 

Thus, if there are not enough of them to occupy the entire space between S and the circumference AEI, everything they do not occupy will be left towards S. 

Since those who press on each other (for example, those in the straight line SE) do not all turn like a rod, but some complete their circuit faster than others, as will be explained further below, the space they leave towards `S` cannot help but be round. 

Even if we imagine more globules initially in the straight line SE than in SA or SI, so that the lowest ones in the line SE are closer to the center S than the lowest ones in the line SI; because the lowest ones complete their circuit faster than the upper ones, some of them would immediately join the end of the line SI to move further away from S; therefore, now all the lowest ones of these lines are equally distant from point S, and thus the space BCD they leave around it is round.

### 62. The same effect causes the celestial matter to try to move away from all points of the circumference of any star or the Sun.

Not only do all the globules in the straight line SE press each other towards E, but each one is also pressed by all the others contained between the straight lines drawn from it to the circumference BCD, which they touch. 

For example, the globule F is pressed by all the others within the lines BF and DF, or the triangular space BFD; not in the same way by the others, so if the place F were empty, all the globules in the space BFD would simultaneously move to fill it as much as possible, but none of the others would. 

Just as we see the same force of gravity that directly leads a falling stone in free air to the center of the Earth also carrying it there obliquely when its direct motion is hindered by the slope of a plane, there is no doubt that the same force by which all the globules in the space `BFD` try to move away from the center S along the straight lines drawn from that center is sufficient to also move them away from it along lines declining from that center.
