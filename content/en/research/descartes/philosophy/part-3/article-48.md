### 48. How all the particles of celestial matter became spherical.

So, to begin to show the efficacy of the laws of nature in the proposed hypothesis, it must be considered that the particles into which we suppose all the matter of this world was initially divided could not have been spherical at first, because several globules joined together do not fill a continuous space. 

But whatever their shape was then, they could not help but become round over time since they had various circular motions. 

For since they were moved at the beginning with sufficient force to separate from each other, that same force, persisting, undoubtedly was also sufficient to wear down all their angles as they subsequently collided. 

For this, not as much force was required as for the initial separation. And from this alone, that the angles of a body are thus worn away, we easily understand that it eventually becomes round. Because here the name "angle" is extended to everything in such a body that protrudes beyond the spherical shape.


### 49. There must be a finer matter around these spherical particles.

However, since spaces devoid of any body cannot exist anywhere, and since those round particles of matter, when joined together, leave some very small gaps around them, these gaps must be filled with some other very tiny fragments of matter, having shapes suited to fill them, and constantly changing according to the ratio of the space occupied. 

Namely, while the angles of those matter particles, which are becoming round, are gradually worn away, what is abraded from them is so minute and acquires such speed that it is divided into innumerable fragments by its own motion. Thus it fills all the angles which other particles of matter cannot penetrate.


### 50. The particles of this finer matter can be easily divided.

The smaller these fragments of other particles are, the more easily they can be moved and broken down into even smaller pieces. 

Because the smaller they are, the more surface area they have in proportion to their volume, and they encounter other bodies by their surface and are divided by their volume.


### 51. The same particles move very quickly.

They are agitated much more swiftly than the other particles of matter from which they acquire their agitation. Because while these other particles move through straight and open paths, they expel the finer particles through oblique and narrow paths. 

In the same way, we see air exit very quickly from a bellows, even if it is slowly closed, because of the narrowness of the passage through which it passes. 

Some portion of matter must move very quickly and be divided into practically infinite parts so that various circular and unequal motions can occur without rarefaction or voids, and no other matter is found to be suitable for this purpose.
