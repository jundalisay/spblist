### 31. How individual planets are carried along. 

Let S be the Sun, and let all the celestial matter surrounding it move in the same direction, namely from the west through the south towards the east, or from A through B towards C, assuming the north pole rises above the plane of this figure, so that the matter around Saturn takes almost thirty years to carry it through its entire circle ♄; whereas the matter around Jupiter carries it through its circle ♃ within twelve years along with its satellites. Thus, Mars completes its circuit in two years, the Earth with the Moon in one year, Venus in eight months, and Mercury in three months, all carried by the matter of the heavens in their respective circles.


### 32. How sunspots are also carried. 

Likewise, certain opaque bodies, visible to us with telescopes and called sunspots, which are contiguous to the Sun's surface, circumnavigate it in a span of twenty-six days.

### 33. How the Earth is also carried around its own center, and the Moon around the Earth.

In water vortices, within this greater vortex of celestial matter, there are smaller vortices, one with Jupiter at its center and another with Earth at its center, which move in the same direction as the greater vortex; and the one with Jupiter at its center carries around it four of its satellites at such speeds that the farthest completes its circuit in 16 days, the next in 7 days, the third in 85 hours, and the one closest to the center in 42 hours; thus, while being carried once around the Sun in the greater circle, they make several smaller circuits around Jupiter.

Similarly, the vortex with Earth at its center causes the Moon to orbit it in a month, while the Earth itself completes a full rotation around its axis each day; so that in the same time the Earth and Moon traverse their common circle once, the Earth rotates 365 times around its center, and the Moon twelve times around the Earth.

### 34. The motions of the heavens are not perfectly circular. 

Finally, we should not think that all the centers of the planets always remain accurately in the same plane, nor that the circles they describe are entirely perfect; but, as we see happening in all other natural things, we should consider them to be approximately such, and to continually change over the centuries.


### 35. On the deviation of the planets in latitude. 

Namely, if this figure represents the plane in which the Earth's center moves throughout the year, called the plane of the Ecliptic, and determined in the heavens by the fixed stars, it must be assumed that each of the other planets moves in some other plane, somewhat inclined to this one, and intersecting it in a line that passes through the Sun's center; so that the Sun is found in all these planes. 

For example, the orbit of Saturn now intersects the Ecliptic in the signs of Cancer and Capricorn, but rises above it, that is, inclines towards the north in Libra, and descends below it towards the south in Aries, with an inclination angle of about 2½ degrees. 

Likewise, the orbits of the other planets intersect the Ecliptic in different places; but the inclination is smaller in Jupiter and Mars, about one degree greater in Venus, and greatest in Mercury, being nearly 7 degrees. Additionally, the Sun's spots (at least if Scheiner S.J.'s observations are accurate, after whose diligence nothing about these spots' phenomena seems lacking) revolve around the Sun in planes inclined by 7 degrees or more to the Ecliptic; so that their motion in this respect does not differ from the motion of the planets. 

The Moon also revolves around the Earth in a plane that deviates by 5 degrees from the Ecliptic; and the Earth revolves around its own axis in the plane of the Equator, deviating by 23½ degrees from the Ecliptic; which plane of the Equator it carries with it. And these deviations of the planets from the Ecliptic are called motions in latitude."
