### 33. All places are full of bodies

The same parts of matter are always being carried along in every motion, and so no body can be moved in a circle. 

For example, body A is moved towards B, only at the same time that part B is moved towards C, C towards D, and D towards A.

It follows that:
- no body expels another from the place it enters
- the place which has been abandoned by the first enters at the same time as it has been abandoned

This we easily understand in a perfect circle, because we see that no emptiness, no rarefaction, or condensation is required for part A of the circle to move towards B, provided that at the same time part B moves towards C, C towards D, and D towards A.

But the same can also be understood for an imperfect and an irrecular circle as long as it is turned in such a way that all the unevenness of the places can be compensated for by the unequal speed of the movements.  

In this way, the whole mass of material contained in space `EFGH` can move in a circle without any condensation or emptiness. At the same time:
- its part facing E can pass towards G
- the one which is facing G can pass towards E.

This is only as long as the space at G is supposed to be 4 times wider than at E, and twice as wide as at F and H, so that it also moves 4 times faster at E than at G, and twice as fast as at F or H. 

And so in all the remaining places the speed of movement compensates for the narrowness of the space. In this way, in any given fixed time, the same amount of material will pass through one part of this circle as through another.


### 34. From this follows the division of matter into really indefinite particles, although they are incomprehensible to us.

In this motion, there is something that our mind perceives to be true, but yet, how it happens is not fully understood. 

That is, the division of certain particles of matter into infinity, or indefinitely, and into so many parts that we cannot conceive of any so small that we understand it to be divided into even smaller ones. 

The matter which already fills space `G` cannot successively fills all the spaces infinitely smaller, which are between `G` and `E` unless some part of it adapts its shape to the countless measures of those spaces. 

For this to occur, all imaginable particles of it, which are indeed innumerable, must be somewhat separated from each other, and any such slight separation constitutes a true division.


### 35. How is this division to be made, and that it cannot be doubted that it is, even if it is not understood.

I am not speaking about the entire matter, but only about some part of it. 

There might be 2 or 3 parts of it in G, as wide as space E, and also several other smaller ones that remain undivided. 

But they move circularly toward E, provided that certain others are mixed with them. 

These bend themselves in various ways and change their shapes in such a manner that, when joined with those not so changing their shapes but adjusting their speed of motion to occupy space accurately, they completely fill all the angles that those others do not occupy. 

Although we may not comprehend by thought how this indefinite division occurs, we should not doubt that it does occur. 

This is because we clearly perceive that it necessarily follows from the nature of matter, which is very evident to us, and we also perceive that it belongs to the class of things that cannot be grasped by our finite mind.
