### 65. The poles of each vortex in the heavens touch parts of other vortices far from their poles.

However the vorticeshave been set in motion at the start, they would now be arranged in such a way that each one moves in the direction where the motions of all the surrounding vortices are least adverse to it. 

This is because the motion of any body can easily be deflected by the encounter with another.

Therefore, if the first vortex, with center S, moves from A through E towards I, then another nearby vortex, with center F, must move from A through E towards V, if no other surrounding vortices hinder them.

Thus, their motions will best agree with each other. 

Similarly, a third vortex, whose center is not in the plane SAFE but above it, forms a triangle with the centers S and F. It is joined to the other two vortices AEI and AEV along the line AE, must move from A through E upwards. 

A fourth vortex, whose center is f, cannot move from E towards I in agreement with the motion of the first, because it would conflict with the motions of the second and third; nor from E towards V, like the second, because it would conflict with the first and third; nor finally from E upwards, like the third, because it would conflict with the first and second. 

Therefore, it must have one of its poles towards E and the other in the opposite part towards B, rotating around the axis EB, from I to V.


### 66. The motions of these vortices are somehow deflected to agree with each other.

There will still be some conflict in these motions if the ecliptics of the first three vortices, that is, the circles farthest from the poles, directly encounter each other at point E, where the pole of the fourth vortex is. 

For example, if IVX is that part of the first vortex around pole E, rotating in a circle according to the order of the notations IVX, the first vortex will brush against it along the straight line EI and its parallels, the second vortex will brush against it along the line EV, and the third along the line EX, which will somewhat oppose its circular motion. 


But nature easily corrects this through the laws of motion by slightly deflecting the ecliptics of the first three vortices towards the direction in which the fourth vortex rotates IVX; so that afterwards they brush against it not along the straight lines EI, EV, EX but along the oblique lines 1I, 2V, 3X, and thus completely agree with its motion.


### 67. The poles of two vortices cannot touch each other

If the poles of two vortices touch each other, either both would move in the same direction and thus merge into one vortex, or in opposite directions and thus conflict with each other as much as possible. 

I do not determine the positions and motions of all the vortices in the heavens.

Nevertheless, the poles of each vortex are not so close to the poles of other contiguous vortices as they are to parts far from their poles.


### 68. These vortices are unequal in size.


The position of the fixed stars have an inexplicable variety. It shows that the vortices revolving around them are not equal to each other. 

However, the fact that no fixed star can exist except at the center of such a vortex is manifest from their light.

Light can be most accurately explained through such vortices, and in no other way without them.

We can only perceive, from the fixed stars, their:
- light
- apparent position.

We have no reason to attribute anything else to them except what we judge necessary to explain these two things. 

But it is no more required to explain their light that vortices of celestial matter revolve around them than it is to explain their apparent position that these vortices be unequal in size. 

I they are unequal, it is necessary that parts of some, far from the poles, touch the parts of others near the poles: because similar parts of greater and lesser vortices cannot be applied to each other.
