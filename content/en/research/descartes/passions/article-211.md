### 211. A general remedy against the Passions

Now that we know them all, we have much less reason to fear them than we did before.

They are all good by nature.

We have nothing to avoid but their bad uses, or their excesses; against which the remedies that I have explained could be sufficient if everyone took enough care to practice them.

But because I have included premeditation among these remedies, and the skill by which one can correct the faults of one's nature by practicing to separate in oneself the movements of the blood and spirits from the thoughts to which they are accustomed to be joined.

There are few people who have prepared themselves in this way against all kinds of encounters, and that these movements excited in the blood by the objects of the Passions follow so quickly from the impressions made in the brain and the disposition of the organs, even though the soul does not contribute in any way, that there is no human wisdom capable of resisting them when one is not sufficiently prepared. 

So many cannot refrain from laughing when tickled, even if they do not take pleasure in it. Because the impression of Joy and surprise, which made them laugh before for the same reason, being awakened in their imagination, causes their lungs to be suddenly inflated despite them, by the blood sent to them by the heart. Thus those who are naturally prone to emotions of Joy, Pity, Fear, or Anger cannot help but faint, or cry, or tremble, or have their blood stirred up, in the same way as if they had a fever, when their imagination is strongly touched by the object of any of these Passions. 

But what one can always do in such a situation, and what I think I can put here as the most general and easiest remedy to practice against all excesses of the Passions, is that when one feels the blood so stirred up, one should be warned and remember that everything that presents itself to the imagination tends to deceive the soul and make it appear that the reasons that persuade the object of its Passion are much stronger than they really are, and those that dissuade, much weaker. And when the Passion persuades only of things that allow for some delay in execution, one must refrain from making any judgment on the spot, and distract oneself with other thoughts until time and rest have completely calmed the emotion in the blood.

When it incites to actions for which it is necessary to make a decision on the spot, the will must mainly focus on considering and following the reasons that are contrary to those presented by the Passion, even if they appear weaker. Like when one is suddenly attacked by an enemy, the situation does not allow for any time to deliberate; but it seems to me that those who are accustomed to reflecting on their actions can always do this, that when they feel seized by Fear, they will try to divert their thoughts from the consideration of the danger by reminding themselves of the reasons why there is much more security and honor in resistance than in flight; and on the contrary, when they feel that the Desire for vengeance and Anger incite them to rashly run towards those who attack them, they will remember to think that it is imprudent to lose oneself when one can escape without dishonor; and that if the odds are very unequal, it is better to make an honorable retreat or surrender, than to recklessly expose oneself to certain death.


### 212. It is from them alone that depends all the good and evil of this life.

The soul can have its own pleasures separately; 

But for those that are common to it with the body, they depend entirely on the Passions, so that those who can be most moved by them are capable of tasting the most sweetness in this life. It is true that they can also find the most bitterness in it, when they do not know how to employ them well, and when fortune is against them. 

But Wisdom is mainly useful in this point, as it teaches to master them so well, and to manage them with such skill, that the ills they cause are very bearable, and even that one can derive joy from all of them.
