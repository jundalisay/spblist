## THE PASSIONS OF THE SOUL

### PART ONE: ON PASSIONS IN GENERAL, AND INCIDENTAL TO THE NATURE OF MAN

**ARTICLE I**
**That what is Passion in relation to one subject is always Action in another respect.**

Nothing better demonstrates the deficiencies of the sciences we have inherited from the ancients than what they have written about the Passions. For although it is a subject whose knowledge has always been highly sought after and seems not among the most difficult to understand, since everyone experiences them within themselves and thus needs no external observation to discover their nature, what the ancients have taught us is so little and, for the most part, so hard to believe, that I cannot hope to approach the truth without deviating from the paths they followed. Therefore, I will be obliged to write here as if treating a subject that no one before me had ever touched upon. To begin, I consider that everything that happens, or occurs anew, is generally called by philosophers a Passion with regard to the subject to which it happens and an Action with regard to the one who causes it to happen. So that although the agent and the patient are often very different, Action and Passion remain always the same thing, which has these two names by reason of the two different subjects to which it can be related.

**ARTICLE II**
**That to know the Passions of the soul, we must distinguish its functions from those of the body.**

I also consider that we do not notice any subject acting more immediately against our soul than the body to which it is joined; and consequently, we must think that what is a Passion in the soul is commonly an Action in the body. Therefore, there is no better way to understand our Passions than by examining the difference between the soul and the body to know to which of the two we should attribute each of the functions that are in us.

**ARTICLE III**
**What rule should be followed for this purpose.**

There will be no great difficulty in this if we notice that everything we experience in ourselves, and that we see can also exist in completely inanimate bodies, must be attributed only to our body; and, conversely, that everything in us which we cannot in any way conceive as belonging to a body must be attributed to our soul.

**ARTICLE IV**
**That heat and movement of the limbs proceed from the body, and thoughts from the soul.**

Thus, because we do not conceive that the body can think in any way, we have reason to believe that all types of thoughts in us belong to the soul; and because we do not doubt that there are inanimate bodies that can move in as many or more diverse ways than ours, and that have as much or more heat (which is evident in the flame, which alone has much more heat and movement than any of our limbs), we must believe that all the heat and all the movements in us, insofar as they do not depend on thought, belong only to the body.

**ARTICLE V**
**That it is an error to believe that the soul gives movement and heat to the body.**

In this way, we will avoid a considerable error into which many have fallen, to the extent that I believe it is the primary cause that has prevented a good explanation of the Passions and other things that belong to the soul up to now. It consists in seeing that all dead bodies are deprived of heat and consequently of movement. People have imagined that it was the absence of the soul that caused these movements and this heat to cease; and thus they have unreasonably believed that our natural heat and all the movements of our bodies depend on the soul. Instead, they should have thought the opposite, that the soul only departs when one dies because this heat ceases and the organs that serve to move the body become corrupted.

**ARTICLE VI**
**What difference there is between a living body and a dead body.**

To avoid this error, consider that death never occurs due to the fault of the soul, but only because some of the principal parts of the body become corrupted; and judge that the body of a living man differs as much from that of a dead man as a clock or other automaton (that is to say, another machine that moves by itself) differs when it is wound up and has in itself the corporeal principle of the movements for which it is designed, along with everything required for its action, and the same clock or other machine when it is broken and the principle of its movement ceases to act.
