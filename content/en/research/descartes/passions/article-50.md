### ARTICLE 45: The Power of the Soul over its Passions

Our passions cannot be directly excited or removed by the action of our will. However, they can be influenced indirectly by representing things that are usually associated with the passions we want to have, and contrary to those we wish to reject. Therefore, to arouse courage in oneself and remove fear, it is not enough to merely will it; one must consider reasons, objects, or examples that persuade one that the danger is not great, that there is always more safety in defense than in flight, that one will gain glory and joy from victory, whereas only regret and shame will come from fleeing, and similar things.

### ARTICLE XLVI: The Reason Preventing the Soul from Fully Controlling its Passions

There is a particular reason why the soul cannot quickly change or stop its passions, which led me to include in their definition that they are not only caused but also maintained and strengthened by certain movements of the spirits. This reason is that they are almost all accompanied by some emotion occurring in the heart and consequently in all the blood and spirits, so that until this emotion ceases, they remain present in our thoughts in the same way that sensory objects remain present while acting against the organs of our senses. Just as the soul, by focusing intensely on something else, can prevent hearing a small noise or feeling a slight pain, but cannot in the same way avoid hearing thunder or feeling the fire burning the hand, it can easily overcome minor passions but not the more violent and strong ones until the emotion of the blood and spirits is calmed. The most the will can do while this emotion is vigorous is not to consent to its effects and to restrain several of the movements it incites in the body. For example, if anger raises the hand to strike, the will can usually hold it back; if fear incites the legs to flee, the will can stop them, and so forth.

### ARTICLE XLVII: On the Conflicts between the Lower and Higher Parts of the Soul

These conflicts, often imagined between the lower part of the soul, called sensitive, and the higher part, which is rational, or between natural appetites and the will, consist only in the opposition between the movements that the body by its spirits, and the soul by its will, tend to excite simultaneously in the gland. For there is only one soul within us, and this soul has no diversity of parts; the same soul that is sensitive is also rational, and all its appetites are wills. The error of attributing different characters to it, often contrary to each other, comes from not clearly distinguishing its functions from those of the body, to which alone we should attribute everything that opposes our reason. Thus, there is no other conflict here than that the small gland in the middle of the brain can be pushed from one side by the soul and from the other by the animal spirits, which are merely bodies, as I have previously said, and it often happens that these two impulses are contrary, and the stronger one prevents the effect of the other. Now, we can distinguish two kinds of movements excited by the spirits in the gland; some represent to the soul the objects that move the senses or the impressions found in the brain and make no effort on its will; others make some effort on it, namely those that cause passions or the movements of the body that accompany them. For the former, even though they often prevent the actions of the soul or are prevented by them, no combat is observed because they are not directly contrary. Combat is only noted between the latter and the wills that oppose them: for example, between the effort with which the spirits push the gland to cause the soul to desire something and the effort with which the soul repels it by the will to avoid the same thing. What primarily makes this combat visible is that the will, lacking the power to directly excite passions, as previously stated, is forced to use ingenuity and apply itself to considering various things successively, so that if one has the strength to momentarily change the course of the spirits, it may happen that the one that follows does not, and they resume immediately afterward because the previous disposition in the nerves, heart, and blood is not changed: this makes the soul feel almost simultaneously driven to desire and not desire the same thing: and this has led to the notion of imagining two powers in the soul that are in conflict. However, one can still conceive of some conflict in that often the same cause that excites a passion in the soul also excites certain movements in the body, to which the soul does not contribute and which it stops or tries to stop as soon as it notices them: as experienced when something that excites fear also causes the spirits to enter the muscles that move the legs to flee, and the will to be brave stops them.

### ARTICLE XLVIII: Knowing the Strength or Weakness of Souls and the Ill of the Weakest

It is through the success of these conflicts that each person can know the strength or weakness of their soul. For those in whom the will can most easily overcome the passions and stop the movements of the body that accompany them, undoubtedly have the strongest souls. But there are those who cannot test their strength because they never let their will fight with its own weapons, only with those provided by some passions to resist others. 

What I call its own weapons are firm and determined judgments concerning the knowledge of good and evil, according to which it has resolved to conduct the actions of its life. 

The weakest souls are those whose will does not determine itself to follow certain judgments but lets itself be continually carried away by present passions, which often oppose each other, drawing the soul alternately to their side and employing it to fight against itself, putting the soul in the most deplorable state it can be in. Thus, when fear represents death as an extreme evil that can only be avoided by flight, if ambition, on the other hand, represents the infamy of this flight as a worse evil than death, these two passions agitate the will differently, which obeying now one, now the other, continuously opposes itself and thus makes the soul a slave and unhappy.



### ARTICLE XLIX: The Strength of the Soul Is Not Enough without the Knowledge of Truth

It is true that there are very few men so weak and irresolute that they only want what their present passion dictates. Most have determined judgments by which they regulate part of their actions. And although these judgments are often false and even based on some passions by which the will has previously been defeated or seduced, because the will continues to follow them when the passion that caused them is absent, they can be considered as its own weapons, and it can be thought that souls are stronger or weaker according to whether they can more or less follow these judgments and resist the present passions that are contrary to them. 

However, there is a great difference between resolutions that come from some false opinion and those based solely on the knowledge of truth: because if one follows the latter, one is assured of never having regret or repentance; whereas one always regrets having followed the former when their error is discovered.

### ARTICLE L: No Soul Is So Weak That It Cannot, When Well Guided, Acquire Absolute Power over Its Passions

And it is useful to know here that, as already said above, although each movement of the gland seems to have been joined by nature to each of our thoughts from the beginning of our life, they can still be joined to others by habit; as experience shows with words that excite movements in the gland, which according to the institution of nature only represent their sound to the soul when spoken or the shape of their letters when written, and yet by the habit acquired in thinking of their meaning when hearing their sound or seeing their letters, they customarily make us conceive this meaning rather than the shape of their letters or the sound of their syllables. 

It is also useful to know that although the movements of both the gland and the spirits and the brain, which represent certain objects to the soul, are naturally joined with those that excite certain passions in it, they can still by habit be separated from them and joined to very different ones; and even this habit can be acquired by a single action and does not require long use. 

Thus, when one unexpectedly encounters something very filthy in food that one eats with appetite, the surprise of this encounter can so change the disposition of the brain that one can no longer see such food afterward without horror, whereas one previously ate it with pleasure.

The same can be noted in animals; for although they have no reason, nor can they have any thought, all the movements of the spirits and the gland that excite passions in us still occur in them and serve to maintain and strengthen not passions, as in us, but the movements of the nerves and muscles that usually accompany them. 

Thus, when a dog sees a partridge, it is naturally inclined to run towards it, and when it hears a gunshot, this noise naturally incites it to flee: but nevertheless, pointing dogs are usually trained so that the sight of a partridge makes them stop, and the noise they hear afterward when the shot is fired makes them run toward it. These things are useful to know to give everyone the courage to study how to regulate their passions. For since one can, with a little skill, change the movements of the brain in animals devoid of reason, it is evident that one can do so even better in humans; and even those with the weakest souls could acquire a very absolute mastery over all their passions if enough skill was employed to train and guide them.
