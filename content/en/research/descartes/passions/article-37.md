### 37. How All Passions Are Caused by Some Movement of the Spirits.

The same occurs in all other passions, namely that they are primarily caused by the spirits contained in the cavities of the brain, as they course towards the nerves that serve to enlarge or constrict the orifices of the heart, or to push the blood towards it from other parts, or in some other way to maintain the same passion: It can be clearly understood from this why I previously stated in their definition that they are caused by some particular movement of the spirits.


### 38. Example of the Movements of the Body That Accompany Passions and Do Not Depend on the Soul.

Furthermore, in the same way that the course these spirits take towards the nerves of the heart is sufficient to give the gland the movement that causes fear in the soul; likewise, merely because some spirits simultaneously go towards the nerves that serve to move the legs to flee, they cause another movement in the same gland, by which the soul feels and perceives this flight, which in this way can be excited in the body solely by the disposition of the organs and without the soul contributing to it.

### ARTICLE XXXIX
#### How the Same Cause Can Excite Various Passions in Different Men.

The same impression that the presence of a frightening object makes on the gland and that causes fear in some men can excite courage and boldness in others. The reason is that not all brains are disposed in the same way; and the same movement of the gland that excites fear in some causes the spirits in others to enter the pores of the brain, guiding them partly to the nerves that serve to move the hands for defense, and partly to those that agitate and push the blood towards the heart in the way required to produce spirits suitable to continue this defense and sustain the will.

### ARTICLE XL
#### What Is the Principal Effect of Passions.

It is necessary to note that the principal effect of all passions in men is that they incite and dispose their soul to want the things they prepare their body for: In such a way that the feeling of fear incites it to want to flee, the feeling of boldness to want to fight, and so on for the others.

### ARTICLE XLI
#### What Is the Power of the Soul with Respect to the Body.

But the will is so free by its nature that it can never be constrained: and of the two kinds of thoughts I have distinguished in the soul, some are its actions, namely its wills, the others are its passions, taking this word in its most general sense, which includes all kinds of perceptions; The former are absolutely within its power and can only be indirectly changed by the body; conversely, the latter depend absolutely on the actions that produce them, and they can only be indirectly changed by the soul, except when it is itself their cause. And all the action of the soul consists in that by merely willing something, it makes the small gland, to which it is closely joined, move in the way required to produce the effect corresponding to that will.

### ARTICLE XLII
#### How One Finds in Memory the Things One Wants to Remember.

Thus, when the soul wants to remember something, this will causes the gland to tilt successively in various directions, pushing the spirits towards different places in the brain until they find the one where the traces left by the object one wants to remember are. For these traces are nothing other than the pores of the brain through which the spirits previously took their course due to the presence of this object, having acquired by this a greater ease than the others in being reopened in the same way by the spirits coming towards them: In such a way that these spirits, finding these pores, enter them more easily than the others, thereby exciting a particular movement in the gland, which represents the same object to the soul and makes it recognize that it is the one it wanted to remember.

### ARTICLE XLIII
#### How the Soul Can Imagine, Be Attentive, and Move the Body.

Thus, when one wants to imagine something never seen before, this will has the power to make the gland move in the way required to push the spirits towards the pores of the brain, through whose opening this thing can be represented. Thus, when one wants to keep one's attention fixed on considering the same object for some time, this will keeps the gland tilted in the same direction for that time. Finally, when one wants to walk or move one's body in some other way, this will makes the gland push the spirits towards the muscles that serve this purpose.


### 44. Each Will Is Naturally Joined to Some Movement of the Gland; But That by Industry or Habit It Can Be Joined to Others.

However, it is not always the will to excite some movement or other effect in us that can make us excite it: but this changes according to how nature or habit has differently joined each movement of the gland to each thought. Thus, for example, if one wants to adjust one's eyes to look at a very distant object, this will makes the pupil dilate; and if one wants to adjust them to look at a very close object, this will makes it contract. But if one thinks only of dilating the pupil, one will not succeed in doing so merely by wanting it: because nature has not joined the movement of the gland that serves to push the spirits towards the optic nerve in the way required to dilate or contract the pupil with the will to dilate or contract it, but with the will to look at distant or close objects. And when speaking, we think only of the meaning of what we want to say, which makes us move the tongue and lips much more quickly and better than if we thought of moving them in all the ways required to pronounce the same words. Because the habit we have acquired in learning to speak has made us join the action of the soul, which through the gland can move the tongue and lips, with the meaning of the words that follow from these movements, rather than with the movements themselves.
